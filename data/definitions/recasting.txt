Recasting in teaching is a pedagogical move in which an instructor reformulates a learner’s utterance to present a more target-like version while preserving the learner’s intended meaning; recasting, as a definition, is the deliberate, immediate, and minimally invasive re-expression of the same proposition with corrected form. Recasting is defined by its dual fidelity: fidelity to meaning and fidelity to improved form, and recasting is anchored in the flow of interaction rather than in a break from it. Recasting is therefore a definition that centers on subtle transformation: the learner’s message remains intact, yet recasting provides a refined linguistic model that can be noticed and internalized. Recasting, as a definitional construct, is best understood as reformulation plus continuity, and recasting is the name we give to that precise coupling.

Recasting operates through a characteristic sequence that clarifies its definition. Recasting begins with attentive listening, because recasting requires an accurate grasp of the learner’s intended meaning. Recasting then selects the smallest necessary formal adjustment to make the utterance more aligned with the target norm, and recasting delivers that adjustment in a natural, conversational tone. Recasting, by definition, is unobtrusive modeling rather than interruption, and recasting presents the improved form as the next conversational turn rather than as an aside.

Recasting is defined not only by what it changes but by when it appears: recasting is immediate, proximal, and context-bound. Recasting occurs right after the learner’s production, and recasting gains its pedagogical force from temporal adjacency. Recasting relies on salience without spectacle: a slight emphasis, a clear articulation, or a measured pace can make recasting perceptible while keeping recasting embedded in the discourse. Recasting thus remains a definition of feedback-as-input, where recasting is the input aligned to the learner’s prior output.

Recasting is applicable across modalities and levels because the definition of recasting is content-agnostic and meaning-preserving. Recasting can define itself in phonology when a pronunciation is refined; recasting can define itself in morphology or syntax when an inflection or word order is adjusted; recasting can define itself in lexis when a more conventional term replaces a nonstandard choice. Recasting scales from brief exchanges to extended academic talk, and recasting remains, by definition, a reformulation that models precision without displacing the learner’s message. Recasting in writing conferences can mirror the same principle orally, and recasting in laboratory or studio settings can anchor technical register while keeping procedures clear.

Recasting hinges on learner noticing, and the definition of recasting includes this potential for uptake without obligating overt repair. Recasting, when noticed, supplies a ready-made template that the learner may adopt in the same interaction, and recasting, when not immediately repeated, can still seed future productions. Recasting is definitionally compatible with gradual consolidation: each instance of recasting incrementally strengthens a mapping between intended meaning and improved form. Recasting, therefore, defines a pathway from exposure to assimilation that respects pacing and attention.

Recasting also carries an affective dimension that is integral to its definition. Recasting maintains conversational harmony, and recasting protects the learner’s agency by affirming the content of the message while refining its form. Recasting reduces the social cost of correction by making the improved form sound like a natural continuation, and recasting promotes confidence by validating what the learner meant to say. Recasting, in this definitional sense, blends care with clarity.

Recasting, as a definition, supports formative observation because recasting creates observable traces in subsequent learner output. Recasting provides moments that educators can code: whether the learner echoes the model, whether recasting leads to immediate adjustment, and whether later turns reflect the influence of recasting. Recasting thus defines a micro-intervention with macro-inferential value about developing competence. Recasting also clarifies instructional intent: by choosing what to reformulate, recasting reveals priorities about accuracy, register, or genre features, and recasting makes those priorities audible.

Recasting, to conclude this definition, is the precise pedagogical act of preserving meaning while refining form through immediate reformulation, and recasting is the name for that gentle, conversational modeling. Recasting is continuous with discourse, tuned to salience, and oriented to learner noticing; recasting is scalable across levels and domains; recasting is evidence-friendly and affect-sensitive. Recasting is, in essence, definition by practice: each instance of recasting enacts the concept itself. Recasting remains a compact description—reformulate, preserve meaning, model improved form—and recasting, repeated consistently, becomes a signature of effective teaching through interaction.
